🍽️ How the Lunch Duty Scheduler Works

A fair, gap‑maximizing algorithm — explained without jargon

The scheduler assigns three lunch‑duty slots (two main rooms + one quiet room) every Monday, Tuesday, and Wednesday. It must honour each staff member’s availability, spread duties evenly, and avoid back‑to‑back assignments. After building a perfectly fair plan, it gently adjusts for pairing preferences (e.g., two teachers who want to work together, or two who should not be on the same day).

⬇️

📐 Phase 1 – Exact Quota Calculation

Goal: Give every person the absolute minimum number of duties required, while respecting their availability.

  1. Count total slots: number of duty days × 3 (because each day needs 3 staff).
  2. Equal division: Divide slots by the number of staff. Most people get base duties; a few randomly chosen people get one extra to cover the remainder.
  3. Availability check: For each person, count how many duty days they can actually work (based on their Monday/Tuesday/Wednesday availability). If someone’s quota exceeds that maximum, it is reduced to a feasible value.
  4. Redistribute excess: Any leftover slots are given to staff who still have capacity, keeping the distribution as equal as possible.
  5. Warnings: If there simply aren’t enough available staff to fill all slots, a warning is raised — but the algorithm still tries its best.
💡 Why “quota”? The system first decides how many duties each person will do before placing them on specific days. This guarantees that the final schedule is fair — nobody is asked to work more than their fair share.
⬇️

⚙️ Phase 2 – Gap‑Maximising Schedule (Greedy Algorithm)

Goal: Place the duties on the actual calendar so that each person’s shifts are spread as far apart as possible, weekday patterns stay varied, and nobody misses their quota.

📖 What is a “greedy” algorithm? It makes the best choice right now, without looking ahead. Imagine picking teammates one by one: each time you choose the person who seems most urgent. That’s greedy. It’s fast, simple, and works well for scheduling when combined with smart scoring.

The scheduler processes every duty day in chronological order (from the first Monday to the last Wednesday). For each day it follows these steps:

1 Build an eligible list
Staff must be available that weekday, still have quota left, and (preferably) not already working in the same calendar week. If not enough eligible staff remain, the “same‑week” rule is relaxed, and in extreme cases the quota is relaxed.

2 Score each candidate
A formula decides who most “needs” a duty today. It values three things:

  • Urgency: how many of their remaining duties must still be fitted in.
  • Gap since last duty: the longer they’ve waited, the higher the score.
  • Weekday balance: avoid always giving the same person Mondays.

A tiny random jitter is added to break ties.
score = 5.0×urgency + 0.55×gap_score + 0.30×balance + 0.15×random

3 Pick the top 3 – the three highest‑scoring eligible staff are chosen for that day. If fewer than three are available, remaining slots are marked “UNASSIGNED”.

4 Assign rooms fairly – the person who has worked the fewest quiet‑room duties so far gets the quiet room. The other two are randomly placed in the two main rooms. This keeps the quiet‑room load balanced over time.

5 Update memory: The scheduler tracks each person’s last duty date, weekday counts, and weekly assignments so that the next day’s scores are accurate.

🎯 Why this works: By always picking people with the longest gaps and highest urgency, duties are naturally spaced out. The weekday‑balance factor makes sure nobody is stuck with every Monday. Over the whole period, everyone ends up with exactly their quota.
⬇️

🔀 Phase 3 – Optional Pairing Swaps (Post‑Processing)

Goal: After the perfectly fair schedule is built, gently adjust it to honour two kinds of tags — should try to pair and should not be paired — without breaking fairness or availability.

🤝 Pro‑Pairing (try to work together)

If two staff members have the “try to pair” tag, the algorithm looks for days where only one of them is on duty. It tries to swap another staff member from a nearby day (within ±14 days) with the tagged person’s partner — but only if:

  • Neither violates their quota.
  • Neither ends up working twice in the same week.
  • Both are available on the swapped day.
  • The swap doesn’t create an anti‑pairing conflict.

🚫 Anti‑Pairing (avoid working together)

If two staff with the “avoid” tag end up on the same day, the algorithm tries to trade one of them with a non‑tagged staff member from a nearby day. Again, the same strict rules apply — no quota changes, no week‑double‑booking, no availability violations.

These swaps are minor and only attempt to improve pairing preferences. They never increase anyone’s duty count or break the fairness built in Phase 2.

⚖️ Fairness first: The swaps are a “best effort” after the main schedule is complete. If a swap would make the schedule unfair or impossible, it is simply skipped. The core promise — everyone does exactly their mathematical share — is always kept.

🧠 In a nutshell: The scheduler first decides how many duties each person must do (Phase 1). Then it places those duties on the calendar, day by day, always choosing the people who have waited the longest and need the most shifts (Phase 2). Finally, it makes small swaps to honour “try to pair” and “avoid pairing” tags, without upsetting the fair distribution (Phase 3). The result is a lunch duty schedule that is mathematically fair, respects availability, and spreads duties as evenly as possible across the term.
